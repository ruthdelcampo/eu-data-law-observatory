## Summary

The Digital Operational Resilience Act (DORA) is a regulation that establishes a comprehensive framework for managing ICT (information and communication technology) risk across the EU financial sector. Enacted on 14 December 2022 and applicable from 17 January 2025, DORA responds to the growing dependence of financial services on digital technology and the systemic risks that ICT failures and cyberattacks pose to financial stability. It replaces a patchwork of national and sectoral rules with uniform, directly applicable requirements for digital operational resilience.

DORA applies to a broad range of financial entities -- over 20 categories in total -- including credit institutions, payment institutions, investment firms, insurance and reinsurance undertakings, crypto-asset service providers, central counterparties, trading venues, and credit rating agencies, among others. Crucially, it also brings ICT third-party service providers (such as cloud providers) within regulatory scope through a dedicated oversight framework for those designated as "critical." The regulation applies proportionally: microenterprises and smaller entities benefit from a simplified ICT risk management regime (Article 16), while larger and systemically important entities face the full set of requirements.

The regulation is structured around five core pillars: (1) ICT risk management, requiring financial entities to maintain comprehensive risk management frameworks with clear governance responsibilities at the management body level; (2) ICT-related incident management, classification, and reporting to competent authorities; (3) digital operational resilience testing, including advanced threat-led penetration testing (TLPT) for significant entities; (4) ICT third-party risk management, imposing contractual requirements and establishing an EU-level oversight framework for critical ICT providers; and (5) information-sharing arrangements that enable financial entities to exchange cyber threat intelligence. Together, these pillars aim to ensure that financial entities can withstand, respond to, and recover from all types of ICT-related disruptions and threats.

DORA is explicitly designated as a sector-specific Union legal act under NIS2 (Directive 2022/2555), meaning that for financial entities falling within both frameworks, DORA's more specific requirements take precedence. It also intersects with the GDPR on personal data protection in the context of incident reporting, and with PSD2 regarding operational and security payment-related incidents.

## Key Provisions

- **Article 1 -- Subject matter**: Defines DORA's scope as establishing uniform requirements for ICT risk management, incident reporting, resilience testing, third-party risk management, and cyber threat information sharing across the financial sector.
- **Article 2 -- Scope**: Lists the 21 categories of financial entities covered (credit institutions, payment institutions, investment firms, insurers, crypto-asset providers, etc.) and the exemptions for certain small entities.
- **Article 4 -- Proportionality principle**: Requires that all obligations be applied proportionally to the entity's size, risk profile, and the nature and complexity of its services and operations.
- **Article 5 -- Governance and organisation**: Places ultimate responsibility for ICT risk management on the management body, which must approve strategies, allocate budgets, and ensure adequate oversight of ICT third-party arrangements.
- **Article 6 -- ICT risk management framework**: Requires financial entities to maintain a sound, comprehensive, and well-documented ICT risk management framework, reviewed at least annually, including a digital operational resilience strategy.
- **Articles 8-10 -- Identification, protection, and detection**: Require entities to identify and classify all ICT assets and business functions, implement security policies (access controls, encryption, change management, patching), and deploy multi-layered detection mechanisms for anomalous activities.
- **Articles 11-12 -- Response, recovery, and backup**: Mandate ICT business continuity plans, response and recovery plans, business impact analyses, and backup policies with physically segregated backup systems and redundant ICT capacities.
- **Article 13 -- Learning and evolving**: Requires post-incident reviews, continuous incorporation of lessons learned into the risk framework, mandatory ICT security awareness training for all staff, and annual reporting to management.
- **Article 16 -- Simplified ICT risk management framework**: Provides a lighter-touch regime for small and non-interconnected investment firms, exempted payment institutions, and small occupational retirement providers.
- **Chapter III -- ICT-related incident reporting**: Establishes classification criteria for major ICT-related incidents and requires timely reporting to competent authorities, including initial, intermediate, and final reports.
- **Chapter IV -- Digital operational resilience testing**: Requires regular testing of ICT tools and systems, and mandates threat-led penetration testing (TLPT) at least every three years for significant financial entities.
- **Chapter V -- ICT third-party risk management**: Imposes mandatory contractual provisions for ICT outsourcing arrangements and creates an EU-level oversight framework for critical ICT third-party service providers, with a Lead Overseer designated from among the ESAs.
- **Chapter VI -- Information-sharing arrangements**: Enables financial entities to establish voluntary arrangements for exchanging cyber threat intelligence and information among themselves, subject to data protection requirements.
