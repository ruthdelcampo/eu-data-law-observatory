## Summary

The Payment Services Directive 2 (PSD2) is a cornerstone of EU financial regulation that modernises the legal framework for payment services across the European Union. Enacted in November 2015 and applicable from January 2018, it replaced the original Payment Services Directive (2007/64/EC) to address the rapid evolution of electronic and mobile payments. PSD2 applies to all payment service providers operating within the Union, including credit institutions, electronic money institutions, payment institutions, and -- critically -- two new categories of regulated providers: payment initiation service providers (PISPs) and account information service providers (AISPs).

The most transformative aspect of PSD2 is its introduction of "open banking." Under Articles 66 and 67, banks and other account servicing payment service providers are required to grant regulated third-party providers access to customer payment accounts, provided the customer gives explicit consent. Payment initiation services (PIS) allow a third party to initiate a payment directly from a user's bank account, while account information services (AIS) enable aggregation of account data from multiple banks into a single interface. Account servicing providers may not discriminate against orders transmitted through these third-party services (Article 66(4)(c)), and no contractual relationship between the third-party provider and the bank is required (Articles 66(5) and 67(4)).

PSD2 also mandates strong customer authentication (SCA) for electronic payments (Article 97). SCA requires at least two independent authentication factors drawn from knowledge (something the user knows), possession (something the user has), and inherence (something the user is). The European Banking Authority (EBA) was tasked with developing regulatory technical standards (Article 98) specifying the details of SCA requirements and the secure communication standards that enable open banking APIs. Together, these provisions aim to increase payment security while fostering competition and innovation in the payments market.

The Directive further strengthens consumer protection by establishing detailed transparency requirements (Title III), capping liability for unauthorised transactions at EUR 50 (Article 74), setting strict execution timelines for payment orders, and creating harmonised complaint and redress procedures. It covers a broad range of payment services -- including credit transfers, direct debits, card payments, and money remittance -- while excluding cash-only transactions, limited-network instruments, and certain low-value payment instruments.

## Key Provisions

- **Article 1 -- Subject matter**: Establishes the categories of regulated payment service providers (credit institutions, e-money institutions, payment institutions, post office giro institutions) and sets the scope of the Directive's rules on transparency and rights/obligations.
- **Article 4 -- Definitions**: Defines key concepts including payment initiation service, account information service, strong customer authentication, sensitive payment data, and personalised security credentials.
- **Articles 5-11 -- Authorisation of payment institutions**: Sets out the conditions and procedures for obtaining authorisation as a payment institution, including initial capital requirements and governance standards.
- **Article 35 -- Access to payment systems**: Requires that access rules for payment systems be proportionate, non-discriminatory, and do not inhibit access beyond what is necessary to safeguard against specific risks.
- **Article 62 -- Charges applicable**: Prohibits surcharging on payment instruments regulated under the Interchange Fee Regulation and allows Member States to prohibit surcharging more broadly.
- **Article 64 -- Consent and withdrawal of consent**: Establishes that payment transactions require the payer's consent, which may be given directly or via a payment initiation service provider, and can be withdrawn at any time before irrevocability.
- **Article 65 -- Confirmation on the availability of funds**: Allows card-issuing payment service providers to request a simple yes/no confirmation of fund availability from the account servicing provider, without disclosing the account balance.
- **Article 66 -- Payment initiation services (PIS)**: Grants payers the right to use payment initiation service providers; requires account servicing providers to grant access and not discriminate against PIS-initiated orders; imposes data minimisation obligations on PISPs.
- **Article 67 -- Account information services (AIS)**: Grants users the right to use account information service providers to aggregate data across accounts; requires explicit consent and restricts data use to the explicitly requested service.
- **Article 68 -- Limits on access**: Allows account servicing providers to deny third-party access only for objectively justified and duly evidenced reasons relating to unauthorised or fraudulent access.
- **Article 73 -- Liability for unauthorised payment transactions**: Requires the payer's payment service provider to immediately refund the amount of an unauthorised payment transaction.
- **Article 74 -- Payer's liability**: Caps the payer's liability for unauthorised transactions at EUR 50 (except in cases of fraud or gross negligence), shifting the main risk to the payment service provider.
- **Article 97 -- Strong customer authentication (SCA)**: Mandates two-factor authentication when the payer accesses a payment account online, initiates an electronic payment, or carries out any action through a remote channel that may imply a risk of payment fraud.
- **Article 98 -- Regulatory technical standards (RTS)**: Delegates to the EBA the development of technical standards specifying SCA requirements, exemptions, and the secure open communication standards between account servicing providers and third-party providers.
