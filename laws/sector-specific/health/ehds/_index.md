## Summary

The European Health Data Space Regulation (EHDS, Regulation 2025/327) establishes a comprehensive EU-wide framework for the access, sharing, and use of electronic health data. It entered into force on 26 March 2025 and is one of the first sector-specific data spaces created under the EU's broader data strategy. The Regulation addresses two fundamentally different modes of health data use: **primary use** (data processed for the direct provision of healthcare to individuals) and **secondary use** (re-use of health data for research, innovation, policymaking, statistics, and public health purposes).

For primary use, the EHDS grants natural persons a set of strong rights over their electronic health data, including the right to access their records free of charge, download copies in a standardised European exchange format, port data between healthcare providers across borders, restrict health professional access, and receive notifications about who has accessed their data. Member States must establish electronic health data access services and designate digital health authorities to enforce these rights. A cross-border infrastructure called **MyHealth@EU** enables the exchange of patient summaries, prescriptions, lab results, and other priority data categories between Member States.

For secondary use, the Regulation creates a governance framework centred on **health data access bodies** designated by each Member State. These bodies evaluate applications from researchers, policymakers, and other legitimate users, issue data permits, and ensure data is processed in secure environments. The Regulation defines 17 categories of electronic health data that must be made available for secondary use -- ranging from EHR data and genomic data to clinical trial data and biobank data -- and strictly limits permitted purposes to public health, research, statistics, education, policymaking, and care improvement. Certain uses are explicitly prohibited, including discrimination based on health data, marketing, and developing harmful products.

The EHDS also imposes requirements on **EHR system manufacturers**, mandating two harmonised software components (a European interoperability component and a European logging component) that must carry CE marking. This connects the Regulation to existing EU product safety and AI legislation. A cross-border infrastructure for secondary use, **HealthData@EU**, mirrors MyHealth@EU and enables multi-country research projects. The Regulation complements and builds upon the GDPR, the Data Governance Act, and the Data Act, while preserving their existing rules on personal data protection and data sharing.

## Key Provisions

- **Article 1 -- Subject matter and scope**: Establishes the EHDS and defines its two pillars (primary use and secondary use), clarifying its relationship with the GDPR, Data Governance Act, Data Act, and medical device regulations.
- **Articles 3-10 -- Patient rights for primary use**: Grant natural persons the right to access their electronic health data, download copies, insert information into their EHR, request rectification, port data between providers and across borders, restrict health professional access, and receive access logs. Member States may allow patients to opt out entirely.
- **Article 14 -- Priority categories of personal electronic health data**: Defines six priority data categories for primary use: patient summaries, electronic prescriptions, electronic dispensations, medical imaging, medical test results, and discharge reports.
- **Article 15 -- European electronic health record exchange format**: Mandates a common, machine-readable format for cross-border exchange of health data, ensuring interoperability between EHR systems across Member States.
- **Article 19 -- Digital health authorities**: Requires each Member State to designate one or more authorities responsible for implementing and enforcing primary use rules, including market surveillance of EHR systems.
- **Article 23 -- MyHealth@EU**: Establishes the cross-border digital infrastructure for exchanging personal electronic health data between national contact points, with the Commission acting as processor.
- **Articles 25-49 -- EHR system requirements (Chapter III)**: Sets mandatory requirements for EHR systems and wellness applications, including two harmonised software components (interoperability and logging), CE marking, conformity assessment, and market surveillance obligations.
- **Article 51 -- Minimum categories of electronic health data for secondary use**: Lists 17 categories of data that health data holders must make available, including EHR data, genomic data, clinical trial data, biobank data, and wellness application data.
- **Article 53 -- Permitted purposes for secondary use**: Limits secondary use to six categories: public health, policymaking, statistics, education, scientific research, and care improvement.
- **Article 54 -- Prohibited secondary use**: Bans using health data for decisions detrimental to individuals, employment discrimination, marketing, developing harmful products, or activities violating ethical provisions.
- **Article 55 -- Health data access bodies**: Requires Member States to designate bodies that manage data permit applications, ensure secure processing environments, and enforce secondary use rules.
- **Article 68 -- Data permits**: Establishes the procedure for health data users to apply for and receive permits to access electronic health data for secondary use, subject to conditions and safeguards.
- **Article 75 -- HealthData@EU**: Creates the cross-border infrastructure for secondary use, connecting national health data access bodies and enabling multi-country research and policy analysis.
