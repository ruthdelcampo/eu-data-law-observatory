## Summary

The Clinical Trials Regulation (Regulation (EU) No 536/2014) is the principal EU framework governing the authorisation, conduct, and oversight of clinical trials on medicinal products for human use. It replaced the earlier Clinical Trials Directive 2001/20/EC with the goal of creating a harmonised, streamlined process across all Member States. The Regulation applies to all interventional clinical trials conducted in the Union and introduces the concept of "low-intervention clinical trials," which benefit from lighter requirements when the investigational medicinal products are already authorised and used within or close to their marketing authorisation terms.

A central innovation of the Regulation is the establishment of the Clinical Trials Information System (CTIS), comprising a single EU portal and EU database managed by the European Medicines Agency. Sponsors submit a single application through this portal, which is then assessed cooperatively by all Member States concerned. This replaced the previous regime of separate national submissions, significantly reducing administrative burden and timelines. The EU database is publicly accessible by default, advancing transparency about clinical research conducted in the Union.

The Regulation places strong emphasis on the protection of trial subjects. It sets out detailed rules on informed consent, requiring that participants receive comprehensive, understandable information before enrolling and that they may withdraw at any time without detriment. Special protections apply to vulnerable populations: clinical trials on minors, incapacitated subjects, pregnant or breastfeeding women, and persons in emergency situations are subject to additional safeguards, including requirements for legally designated representatives and direct benefit thresholds. The overarching principle is that the rights, safety, dignity, and well-being of subjects always prevail over all other interests.

Safety reporting is another major pillar. The Regulation establishes a structured system whereby investigators report adverse events to sponsors, who in turn report suspected unexpected serious adverse reactions (SUSARs) to the EMA's Eudravigilance database. This centralised approach ensures that safety signals are detected quickly across all Member States, enabling coordinated risk assessment. The Regulation also addresses damage compensation, manufacturing and labelling of investigational products, good clinical practice, and Member State cooperation in inspections and supervision.

## Key Provisions

- **Article 1 -- Scope**: The Regulation applies to all clinical trials conducted in the Union; it does not apply to non-interventional studies.
- **Article 2 -- Definitions**: Establishes key definitions including "clinical trial," "low-intervention clinical trial," "investigational medicinal product," "informed consent," "sponsor," and "good clinical practice."
- **Article 3 -- General principle**: A clinical trial may be conducted only if the rights, safety, dignity, and well-being of subjects are protected and it is designed to generate reliable and robust data.
- **Articles 4-14 -- Authorisation procedure**: Creates a single submission procedure through the EU portal, with coordinated assessment by a reporting Member State and all Member States concerned, and strict timelines for decisions.
- **Articles 15-24 -- Substantial modifications**: Sets out the procedure for authorising significant changes to an ongoing clinical trial.
- **Articles 25-27 -- Application dossier**: Specifies the documentation required for clinical trial applications, including the protocol, investigator's brochure, and manufacturing information.
- **Articles 28-35 -- Protection of subjects and informed consent**: Core subject-protection provisions requiring written informed consent, comprehensive information disclosure, and the right to withdraw at any time. Includes special rules for incapacitated subjects (Article 31), minors (Article 32), pregnant or breastfeeding women (Article 33), and emergency situations (Article 35).
- **Articles 36-39 -- Start, end, and halt of trials**: Rules on notifying the start and end of trials, temporary halts, and early termination, including obligations to report reasons.
- **Articles 40-43 -- Safety reporting**: Establishes the electronic safety reporting system through Eudravigilance, with investigators reporting adverse events to sponsors and sponsors reporting SUSARs to the Agency within strict timeframes.
- **Article 44 -- Annual safety reporting**: Requires sponsors to submit annual safety reports to the Agency and Member States concerned.
- **Articles 47-51 -- Conduct of trials and good clinical practice**: Sets requirements for trial conduct, including the role of the investigator, sponsor oversight, and compliance with good clinical practice.
- **Articles 61-62 -- Manufacturing and import**: Regulates the manufacturing, import, and quality assurance of investigational medicinal products.
- **Article 76 -- Damage compensation**: Requires Member States to ensure systems are in place for compensation of subjects who suffer damage from participation in a clinical trial.
- **Articles 80-81 -- EU portal and EU database (CTIS)**: Establishes the single EU portal for submissions and the publicly accessible EU database, managed by the European Medicines Agency, which assigns unique EU trial numbers and enables transparency.
- **Article 82 -- Public accessibility**: Requires that the EU database be publicly accessible, subject to limited exceptions for personal data protection, commercial confidentiality, and effective supervision.
