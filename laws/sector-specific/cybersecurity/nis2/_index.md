## Summary

The Network and Information Security Directive 2 (NIS2), formally Directive (EU) 2022/2555, is a comprehensive EU cybersecurity law that replaces and significantly expands the original NIS Directive (2016/1148). Enacted on 14 December 2022 and applicable from 17 October 2024, NIS2 aims to achieve a high common level of cybersecurity across the Union by establishing harmonised requirements for cybersecurity risk management, incident reporting, and supervisory enforcement. It addresses the fragmented and uneven implementation of the original directive by broadening the scope of covered entities and introducing stricter obligations.

NIS2 applies to medium-sized and large entities across a wide range of sectors listed in its annexes, including energy, transport, banking, financial market infrastructure, health, drinking water, wastewater, digital infrastructure, ICT service management, public administration, and space (Annex I -- "essential" sectors), as well as postal services, waste management, chemicals, food, manufacturing, digital providers, and research (Annex II -- "important" sectors). Certain entities, such as trust service providers, DNS service providers, and top-level domain registries, fall within scope regardless of size.

The directive introduces a two-tier classification of entities -- essential and important -- which determines the intensity of supervisory measures they face. All covered entities must implement cybersecurity risk-management measures based on an all-hazards approach (Article 21), covering policies on risk analysis, incident handling, business continuity, supply chain security, vulnerability management, cryptography, access control, and multi-factor authentication. Management bodies must approve and oversee these measures and can be held personally liable for non-compliance.

A central pillar of NIS2 is its incident reporting framework (Article 23): entities must submit an early warning to their national CSIRT or competent authority within 24 hours of becoming aware of a significant incident, followed by a full incident notification within 72 hours and a final report within one month. The directive also strengthens cooperation mechanisms at EU level through the Cooperation Group, the CSIRTs network, and the newly established EU-CyCLONe (Cyber Crisis Liaison Organisation Network) for coordinated management of large-scale cybersecurity incidents.

## Key Provisions

- **Article 1 -- Subject matter**: Establishes the directive's purpose of achieving a high common level of cybersecurity across the Union, including obligations for national cybersecurity strategies, risk-management measures, reporting, and enforcement.
- **Article 2 -- Scope**: Defines the entities covered, primarily medium-sized and large enterprises in sectors listed in Annexes I and II, with certain entities (DNS providers, trust service providers, TLD registries) included regardless of size.
- **Article 3 -- Essential and important entities**: Introduces the two-tier classification system that determines supervisory intensity. Essential entities include large enterprises in Annex I sectors; important entities are medium-sized enterprises in Annex I or II sectors.
- **Article 7 -- National cybersecurity strategy**: Requires each Member State to adopt a national cybersecurity strategy addressing supply chain security, vulnerability management, cyber hygiene, and workforce development.
- **Article 9 -- CSIRTs**: Requires Member States to designate or establish Computer Security Incident Response Teams with adequate resources and technical capabilities.
- **Article 14 -- Cooperation Group**: Establishes a strategic cooperation body composed of Member State representatives, the Commission, and ENISA to guide policy implementation and information exchange.
- **Article 16 -- EU-CyCLONe**: Creates the European Cyber Crises Liaison Organisation Network to coordinate the management of large-scale cross-border cybersecurity incidents at the operational level.
- **Article 20 -- Governance**: Requires management bodies to approve and oversee cybersecurity risk-management measures, mandates management training, and establishes personal liability for non-compliance.
- **Article 21 -- Cybersecurity risk-management measures**: Mandates an all-hazards approach covering at least ten domains: risk analysis, incident handling, business continuity, supply chain security, vulnerability management, effectiveness assessment, cyber hygiene and training, cryptography, access control, and multi-factor authentication.
- **Article 22 -- Coordinated supply chain risk assessments**: Empowers the Cooperation Group, with ENISA and the Commission, to carry out EU-level coordinated risk assessments of critical ICT supply chains.
- **Article 23 -- Reporting obligations**: Establishes the tiered incident reporting timeline: early warning within 24 hours, incident notification within 72 hours, and a final report within one month.
- **Article 32 -- Supervisory and enforcement measures for essential entities**: Grants authorities powers including on-site inspections, security audits, and the ability to issue binding instructions, with administrative fines of up to EUR 10 million or 2% of worldwide annual turnover.
- **Article 33 -- Supervisory and enforcement measures for important entities**: Provides ex-post supervisory powers for important entities, with administrative fines of up to EUR 7 million or 1.4% of worldwide annual turnover.
- **Article 34 -- General conditions for administrative fines**: Sets out factors to be considered when imposing fines, including the gravity and duration of the infringement, previous violations, and the degree of cooperation.
