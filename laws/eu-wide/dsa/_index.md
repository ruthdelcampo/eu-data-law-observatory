## Summary

The Digital Services Act (DSA) is an EU regulation that establishes a comprehensive framework governing the responsibilities of online intermediary services, including internet service providers, hosting services, online platforms, and search engines. Its overarching goal is to create a safer and more transparent digital environment across the European Union while preserving innovation and protecting fundamental rights, including freedom of expression and consumer protection. The regulation applies to any intermediary service offered to recipients located in the EU, regardless of where the provider is established.

The DSA introduces a tiered system of obligations that scale with the size and societal impact of the service. All intermediary services must designate points of contact, publish transparency reports, and comply with orders from national authorities regarding illegal content. Hosting services face additional requirements, including notice-and-action mechanisms that allow users to flag illegal content, obligations to provide statements of reasons when restricting content, and a duty to notify law enforcement of suspected criminal offences threatening life or safety. Online platforms must additionally maintain internal complaint-handling systems, cooperate with certified out-of-court dispute settlement bodies, grant priority to trusted flaggers, and implement safeguards against misuse of their systems.

The most stringent obligations fall on very large online platforms (VLOPs) and very large online search engines (VLOSEs), defined as those with 45 million or more monthly active users in the EU. These entities must conduct systemic risk assessments covering the dissemination of illegal content, negative effects on fundamental rights, public health, civic discourse, and electoral processes. They are required to implement risk mitigation measures, submit to independent audits, provide data access to researchers, maintain publicly accessible advertisement repositories, and offer users at least one recommender system option that is not based on profiling.

Enforcement is shared between national Digital Services Coordinators and the European Commission. Each Member State must designate an independent Digital Services Coordinator with investigation and enforcement powers, including the ability to impose fines of up to 6% of a provider's annual worldwide turnover. The Commission holds exclusive supervisory powers over VLOPs and VLOSEs for their additional obligations and may impose fines of up to 6% of global turnover as well. A European Board for Digital Services facilitates coordination across Member States. Recipients of services have the right to lodge complaints and seek compensation for damages resulting from providers' non-compliance.

## Key Provisions

- **Article 1 -- Subject matter**: Sets out the DSA's dual aim of ensuring the proper functioning of the internal market for intermediary services and creating a safe, transparent online environment that protects fundamental rights.
- **Article 3 -- Definitions**: Defines core concepts including intermediary service (mere conduit, caching, hosting), online platform, online search engine, illegal content, content moderation, recommender system, and advertisement.
- **Articles 4-6 -- Liability exemptions**: Preserves the conditional liability safe harbours for mere conduit, caching, and hosting providers, carried over and updated from the e-Commerce Directive.
- **Article 8 -- No general monitoring obligation**: Prohibits imposing general surveillance obligations on intermediary services, a foundational principle limiting proactive content scanning duties.
- **Article 9 -- Orders to act against illegal content**: Establishes a harmonised framework for judicial and administrative orders requiring providers to act against specific illegal content.
- **Article 14 -- Terms and conditions**: Requires providers to clearly disclose content moderation policies, including the use of algorithmic decision-making, in plain and accessible language.
- **Article 15 -- Transparency reporting**: Mandates annual public reports on content moderation activities, including the use of automated tools, orders received, and notices processed.
- **Article 16 -- Notice and action mechanisms**: Obliges hosting services to implement user-friendly systems for reporting illegal content, with procedural safeguards and timely responses.
- **Article 17 -- Statement of reasons**: Requires hosting services to provide clear, specific justifications to users when restricting their content or accounts, including information on available redress.
- **Article 20 -- Internal complaint-handling system**: Requires online platforms to offer a free, accessible internal complaints process for users affected by content moderation decisions.
- **Article 22 -- Trusted flaggers**: Creates a status for specialised entities whose reports of illegal content must be treated with priority by platforms.
- **Article 26 -- Advertising transparency**: Requires online platforms to ensure that advertisements are clearly identifiable and to disclose information about the advertiser and targeting parameters.
- **Article 33 -- Very large online platforms and search engines**: Defines VLOPs and VLOSEs as those reaching 45 million or more monthly active users in the EU, triggering the most demanding compliance tier.
- **Article 34 -- Risk assessment**: Requires VLOPs and VLOSEs to identify and analyse systemic risks, including illegal content dissemination, impacts on fundamental rights, public health, and electoral integrity.
- **Article 35 -- Risk mitigation**: Obliges VLOPs and VLOSEs to adopt reasonable, proportionate measures to mitigate identified systemic risks.
- **Article 37 -- Independent audits**: Mandates annual independent audits of VLOP and VLOSE compliance with their DSA obligations.
- **Article 38 -- Recommender systems**: Requires VLOPs and VLOSEs to offer users at least one recommender system option not based on profiling.
- **Article 39 -- Online advertising transparency**: Requires VLOPs and VLOSEs to maintain publicly accessible, searchable repositories of all advertisements displayed on their services.
- **Article 40 -- Data access for researchers**: Obliges VLOPs and VLOSEs to provide vetted researchers with access to data necessary for studying systemic risks.
- **Article 49 -- Digital Services Coordinators**: Requires each Member State to designate an independent authority responsible for supervising intermediary services and enforcing the DSA.
- **Article 52 -- Penalties**: Sets the maximum fine at 6% of annual worldwide turnover for infringements and 1% for providing incorrect or misleading information during investigations.
