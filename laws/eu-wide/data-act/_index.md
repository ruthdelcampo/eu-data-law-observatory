## Summary

The Data Act (Regulation (EU) 2023/2854) establishes harmonised rules on access to and use of data generated by connected products and related services across the EU. It covers both personal and non-personal data. The regulation creates a right for users of IoT devices and related digital services to access product data and to share that data with third parties of their choice, while imposing corresponding obligations on data holders (typically manufacturers and service providers) to make such data available under fair, reasonable, and non-discriminatory (FRAND) terms.

The regulation applies to manufacturers of connected products placed on the EU market, users of those products, data holders, data recipients, public sector bodies, and providers of data processing services (cloud/edge). It explicitly excludes micro and small enterprises from most data-sharing obligations under Chapter II and from certain public-sector data requests. Gatekeepers designated under the Digital Markets Act are prohibited from receiving data shared under the user's right to share with third parties.

Key mechanisms include: mandatory by-design accessibility of IoT-generated data (Art. 3); user rights to access and share data with third parties (Arts. 4-5); FRAND compensation terms for B2B data sharing with cost-based caps for SMEs (Art. 9); a fairness test for unilaterally imposed contract terms in B2B data access agreements (Art. 13); and a framework for public sector bodies to request private-sector data in cases of exceptional need such as public emergencies (Arts. 14-21). The regulation also addresses cloud switching by requiring providers to eliminate switching charges by January 2027, ensure data portability, and support functional equivalence between services (Arts. 23-31).

Enforcement is decentralised to Member State competent authorities, with each state designating one or more authorities and, where multiple exist, a data coordinator. GDPR supervisory authorities retain jurisdiction over personal data aspects. Penalties must be effective, proportionate, and dissuasive; for obligations under Chapters II, III, and V involving personal data, fines may reach the levels set in GDPR Article 83(5) (up to EUR 20 million or 4% of global turnover). The European Data Innovation Board (EDIB) supports consistent cross-border application.

The regulation also limits the sui generis database right under Directive 96/9/EC, clarifying that it does not apply to data obtained from or generated by connected products falling within the Data Act's scope (Art. 43). This prevents data holders from using database rights to circumvent the regulation's data-sharing obligations.

## Key Provisions

- **Article 1 -- Subject matter and scope.** Defines the six regulatory pillars: IoT data access, B2B data sharing, public sector data access, cloud switching, safeguards against unlawful third-country access, and interoperability standards.
- **Article 3 -- Data accessibility by design.** Requires connected products and related services to be designed so that product data is accessible to the user by default, in a structured, machine-readable format, free of charge.
- **Article 4 -- User access rights.** Obliges data holders to make readily available data accessible to users without undue delay, at the same quality available to the holder, free of charge. Includes trade secret protections and dispute mechanisms.
- **Article 5 -- Right to share data with third parties.** Users may direct data holders to share their IoT data with third parties. Gatekeepers under the DMA are excluded as eligible recipients.
- **Article 6 -- Third party obligations.** Third parties receiving data may use it only for agreed purposes, must not develop competing products, and must not share data with gatekeepers.
- **Article 8 -- FRAND terms for B2B data sharing.** Data holders obliged to share data must do so under fair, reasonable, non-discriminatory, and transparent conditions.
- **Article 9 -- Compensation.** B2B compensation must be reasonable and non-discriminatory. For SMEs and not-for-profit research organisations, compensation is capped at direct costs of making data available.
- **Article 13 -- Unfair contract terms.** Contractual terms on data access unilaterally imposed by one enterprise on another are not binding if they grossly deviate from good commercial practice. Lists terms that are automatically unfair and terms that are presumed unfair.
- **Articles 14-15 -- Public sector data access.** Public sector bodies may request private-sector data only on the basis of an exceptional need (public emergency or specific statutory task), subject to strict procedural requirements.
- **Article 20 -- Compensation for public sector access.** Data to respond to public emergencies must be provided free of charge (except by micro/small enterprises). For other exceptional needs, fair compensation covering costs plus a reasonable margin is required.
- **Article 23 -- Cloud switching obligations.** Cloud and data processing service providers must remove commercial, technical, and contractual obstacles to switching, including supporting data portability and functional equivalence.
- **Article 29 -- Elimination of switching charges.** Switching charges must be fully eliminated by 12 January 2027, with only reduced cost-based charges permitted during the transition period (2024-2027).
- **Article 37 -- Competent authorities.** Each Member State designates competent authorities for enforcement. GDPR supervisory authorities handle personal data aspects. Authorities may impose financial penalties including periodic penalties.
- **Article 40 -- Penalties.** Member States set penalty rules. For personal data obligations under Chapters II, III, and V, fines may reach GDPR Article 83(5) levels.
- **Article 43 -- Sui generis database right limitation.** The sui generis right under Directive 96/9/EC does not apply to data from connected products within the Data Act's scope.
