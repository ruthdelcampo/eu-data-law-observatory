## Summary

The Data Governance Act (DGA) is an EU regulation that establishes a framework to facilitate data sharing across the European Union while preserving trust and control for data holders and data subjects. Adopted on 30 May 2022 and applicable from 24 September 2023, it aims to increase the availability of data for use by strengthening mechanisms for data re-use, intermediation, and altruism. The DGA is a cornerstone of the European Strategy for Data and complements the GDPR by creating structures that make voluntary data sharing easier and more trustworthy, without altering existing data protection rights.

The regulation addresses three core pillars. First, it sets conditions for the re-use of certain categories of protected data held by public sector bodies, including commercially confidential data, data protected by intellectual property rights, and personal data that falls outside the scope of the Open Data Directive. Public sector bodies that permit re-use must ensure non-discriminatory, transparent conditions and may require the use of secure processing environments, anonymisation, or pseudonymisation techniques to safeguard the protected nature of the data.

Second, the DGA creates a notification and supervisory framework for data intermediation services -- neutral third-party providers that facilitate data sharing between data holders and data users. These intermediaries must operate under strict neutrality conditions: they cannot use the data they intermediate for their own purposes, must provide services through a separate legal entity, and must act in the best interest of data subjects. This framework seeks to build trust in data-sharing ecosystems by ensuring that intermediaries do not exploit their position.

Third, the regulation introduces a voluntary registration regime for data altruism organisations -- not-for-profit entities that collect and make data available for objectives of general interest such as scientific research, healthcare improvement, or combating climate change. Registered organisations may use the label "data altruism organisation recognised in the Union" and must comply with transparency and safeguarding requirements laid down in a Commission-adopted rulebook.

Finally, the DGA establishes the European Data Innovation Board (EDIB), an expert group composed of representatives from Member State competent authorities and other stakeholders. The EDIB advises the Commission on consistent practices for data re-use, data intermediation, and data altruism, and promotes cross-sector interoperability and the development of common European data spaces. The regulation also includes provisions on international data transfers, requiring safeguards to prevent transfers of non-personal data that would conflict with EU law.

## Key Provisions

- **Article 1** -- Subject matter and scope: defines the four pillars of the regulation (re-use of public sector data, data intermediation services, data altruism, and the European Data Innovation Board) and clarifies that the DGA does not override GDPR or other data protection law.
- **Article 2** -- Definitions: establishes key concepts including "data intermediation service," "data altruism," "data holder," "data user," "re-use," and "secure processing environment."
- **Articles 3-9** -- Re-use of protected public sector data: sets out which categories of protected data are covered (commercial confidentiality, statistical confidentiality, IP rights, personal data), prohibits exclusive arrangements, defines conditions for re-use (including anonymisation, secure processing environments, and fee structures), and requires Member States to designate competent bodies and single information points.
- **Articles 10-15** -- Data intermediation services: defines the types of intermediation services covered, establishes a notification procedure, and lays down conditions including structural separation, neutrality, interoperability, and data subject protection. Competent authorities monitor compliance and may impose penalties including cessation of services.
- **Articles 16-25** -- Data altruism: creates a voluntary registration framework for not-for-profit organisations collecting data for general interest objectives, with transparency requirements, safeguards for data subjects and data holders, a Commission-adopted rulebook, and a European data altruism consent form.
- **Articles 26-28** -- Competent authorities and procedural provisions: requires authorities to be independent and adequately resourced, and guarantees rights to lodge complaints and seek effective judicial remedies.
- **Articles 29-30** -- European Data Innovation Board: establishes the EDIB and defines its advisory tasks on data re-use practices, interoperability standards, cybersecurity guidelines, and common European data spaces.
- **Article 31** -- International access and transfer: requires reasonable measures to prevent international transfers of non-personal data that conflict with EU law, with specific rules for third-country court orders and government access requests.
- **Article 34** -- Penalties: requires Member States to lay down effective, proportionate, and dissuasive penalties for infringements.
