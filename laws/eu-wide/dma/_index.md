## Summary

The Digital Markets Act (DMA) is an EU regulation that establishes harmonised rules to ensure contestable and fair markets in the digital sector across the European Union. It targets large online platforms that act as "gatekeepers" -- companies that operate core platform services such as search engines, app stores, messaging services, operating systems, social networks, cloud computing, and online advertising, and that hold a position of significant and durable market power. The regulation aims to prevent these gatekeepers from imposing unfair conditions on business users and end users while also keeping digital markets open to competition and innovation.

Under the DMA, an undertaking is designated as a gatekeeper if it has a significant impact on the internal market, provides a core platform service that serves as an important gateway for business users to reach end users, and enjoys an entrenched and durable market position. Quantitative thresholds create a presumption of gatekeeper status: an annual EU turnover of at least EUR 7.5 billion (or market capitalisation of EUR 75 billion), combined with at least 45 million monthly active end users and 10,000 yearly active business users in the EU, maintained over three consecutive years. The European Commission is responsible for designating gatekeepers and can also designate companies that fall below these thresholds through a qualitative market investigation.

Once designated, gatekeepers must comply with a set of obligations within six months. These include prohibitions on combining personal data across services without explicit user consent, bans on anti-competitive self-preferencing in rankings, requirements to allow side-loading of third-party apps and alternative app stores, obligations to provide data portability for end users, and duties to ensure interoperability of messaging services. Gatekeepers must also refrain from preventing business users from offering different prices on competing platforms, and they must share advertising performance data with advertisers and publishers.

The DMA grants the Commission strong enforcement and market investigation powers. It can conduct market investigations to designate new gatekeepers, examine systematic non-compliance, and identify emerging practices that limit contestability. In cases of systematic infringement, the Commission may impose behavioural or structural remedies, including temporarily prohibiting a gatekeeper from making acquisitions. Gatekeepers are also required to notify the Commission of all intended concentrations involving digital services, regardless of whether EU merger thresholds are met.

The regulation works alongside existing EU competition law (Articles 101 and 102 TFEU) and data protection legislation (GDPR, ePrivacy Directive), and it complements the Digital Services Act (DSA). Member States are prohibited from imposing additional obligations on gatekeepers for the purpose of ensuring contestable and fair markets, ensuring a single harmonised regulatory framework across the EU.

## Key Provisions

- **Article 2 -- Definitions**: Defines core platform services (including online intermediation, search engines, social networks, messaging, operating systems, browsers, virtual assistants, cloud, and advertising), gatekeepers, business users, end users, interoperability, and other key terms.
- **Article 3 -- Designation of gatekeepers**: Establishes the three-part test for gatekeeper status and the quantitative thresholds (EUR 7.5 billion turnover or EUR 75 billion market cap; 45 million monthly end users and 10,000 yearly business users). Requires gatekeepers to comply with obligations within 6 months of designation.
- **Article 5 -- Obligations for gatekeepers**: Prohibits combining personal data across services without consent, prevents anti-steering clauses (business users may offer different prices elsewhere), requires allowing end users to access content purchased outside the platform, and mandates advertising transparency for both advertisers and publishers.
- **Article 6 -- Obligations susceptible of further specification**: Bans use of non-public business-user data for competition against those users, requires allowing app side-loading and alternative app stores, prohibits self-preferencing in rankings, mandates data portability for end users, requires real-time data access for business users, and obliges sharing of search data with third-party search engines on FRAND terms.
- **Article 7 -- Interoperability of messaging services**: Requires gatekeepers to make their number-independent interpersonal communications services interoperable with those of other providers, with phased timelines for text messaging, group messaging, and voice/video calls, while preserving end-to-end encryption.
- **Article 13 -- Anti-circumvention**: Prohibits gatekeepers from undermining their obligations through technical, contractual, or behavioural means, including degrading service quality for users who exercise their rights under the DMA.
- **Article 14 -- Obligation to inform about concentrations**: Requires gatekeepers to notify the Commission of all intended mergers and acquisitions involving digital services or data-collection activities, regardless of whether EU or national merger notification thresholds are met.
- **Article 18 -- Market investigation into systematic non-compliance**: Empowers the Commission to impose behavioural or structural remedies, including temporary acquisition bans, where a gatekeeper has received at least three non-compliance decisions within eight years.
