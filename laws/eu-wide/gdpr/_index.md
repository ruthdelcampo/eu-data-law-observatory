## Summary

The General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) is the EU's principal law governing the processing of personal data. Adopted in 2016 and enforceable since 25 May 2018, it replaced the 1995 Data Protection Directive (95/46/EC) with a directly applicable regulation that harmonises data protection rules across all EU/EEA Member States. Its dual objective is to protect natural persons' fundamental right to data protection while ensuring the free movement of personal data within the internal market.

The GDPR applies to any organisation -- whether established in the EU or not -- that processes personal data of individuals located in the EU, provided the processing relates to offering goods or services to those individuals or monitoring their behaviour within the EU (Art. 3). "Personal data" is defined broadly to include any information relating to an identified or identifiable natural person, and "processing" covers virtually every operation performed on such data. The regulation distinguishes between controllers (who determine the purposes and means of processing) and processors (who act on behalf of controllers), imposing specific obligations on each.

At its core, the GDPR establishes six principles for lawful processing: lawfulness, fairness and transparency; purpose limitation; data minimisation; accuracy; storage limitation; and integrity and confidentiality (Art. 5). Processing requires a legal basis, the most common being consent, contractual necessity, legal obligation, vital interests, public interest, or legitimate interests (Art. 6). Special categories of data -- including racial or ethnic origin, health data, biometric data, and political opinions -- receive heightened protection under Art. 9 and may only be processed under narrow exceptions.

Data subjects are granted enforceable rights including access (Art. 15), rectification (Art. 16), erasure/"right to be forgotten" (Art. 17), data portability (Art. 20), objection to processing (Art. 21), and the right not to be subject to solely automated decisions with legal or similarly significant effects (Art. 22). Controllers must provide transparent information about processing activities, respond to data subject requests within one month, and implement data protection by design and by default (Art. 25). Organisations processing data on a large scale or handling sensitive data must appoint a Data Protection Officer (Art. 37) and may be required to conduct Data Protection Impact Assessments (Art. 35).

Enforcement rests with independent national supervisory authorities (Data Protection Authorities) in each Member State, coordinated through the European Data Protection Board. Administrative fines can reach up to 20 million EUR or 4% of total worldwide annual turnover, whichever is higher, for the most serious infringements -- such as violations of processing principles, data subject rights, or international transfer rules (Art. 83). Data subjects also have the right to lodge complaints, seek judicial remedies against controllers or processors, and claim compensation for material or non-material damage (Arts. 77-82). International transfers of personal data require adequacy decisions, appropriate safeguards such as standard contractual clauses or binding corporate rules, or narrow derogations (Arts. 44-49).

## Key Provisions

- **Art. 3 -- Territorial scope.** Extends the regulation to non-EU controllers and processors that target or monitor individuals in the EU.
- **Art. 5 -- Principles.** Sets out the six core principles: lawfulness/fairness/transparency, purpose limitation, data minimisation, accuracy, storage limitation, integrity/confidentiality, plus the accountability principle.
- **Art. 6 -- Lawfulness of processing.** Enumerates the six legal bases for processing: consent, contract, legal obligation, vital interests, public interest, and legitimate interests.
- **Art. 7 -- Conditions for consent.** Requires consent to be freely given, specific, informed, and unambiguous; must be as easy to withdraw as to give.
- **Art. 9 -- Special categories of data.** Prohibits processing of sensitive data (racial/ethnic origin, health, biometrics, political opinions, etc.) except under enumerated exceptions.
- **Art. 13-14 -- Transparency obligations.** Mandates disclosure of controller identity, processing purposes, legal basis, retention periods, and data subject rights at the time of data collection.
- **Art. 15 -- Right of access.** Grants data subjects the right to obtain confirmation of processing and a copy of their personal data.
- **Art. 17 -- Right to erasure.** Establishes the "right to be forgotten" when data is no longer necessary, consent is withdrawn, or processing is unlawful.
- **Art. 20 -- Right to data portability.** Allows data subjects to receive their data in a structured, machine-readable format and transmit it to another controller.
- **Art. 22 -- Automated decision-making.** Provides the right not to be subject to decisions based solely on automated processing, including profiling, that produce legal or similarly significant effects.
- **Art. 25 -- Data protection by design and by default.** Requires controllers to implement technical and organisational measures (e.g., pseudonymisation, data minimisation) from the design stage.
- **Art. 28 -- Processor obligations.** Requires a binding contract between controller and processor specifying processing terms, security measures, and sub-processor conditions.
- **Art. 33-34 -- Breach notification.** Mandates notification of personal data breaches to the supervisory authority within 72 hours and to affected data subjects when there is high risk.
- **Art. 35 -- Data Protection Impact Assessment.** Requires prior assessment of high-risk processing operations, particularly those involving new technologies, large-scale profiling, or systematic monitoring.
- **Art. 37-39 -- Data Protection Officer.** Requires designation of a DPO for public authorities, large-scale monitoring, and large-scale processing of sensitive data.
- **Art. 44-49 -- International transfers.** Permits transfers to third countries only on the basis of adequacy decisions, appropriate safeguards (SCCs, BCRs, codes of conduct, certification), or specific derogations.
- **Art. 77-79 -- Remedies.** Grants data subjects the right to lodge complaints with supervisory authorities and to bring judicial proceedings against controllers, processors, or supervisory authorities.
- **Art. 82 -- Compensation.** Establishes the right to compensation for material or non-material damage resulting from GDPR infringements.
- **Art. 83 -- Administrative fines.** Sets two tiers: up to 10M EUR / 2% turnover for organisational violations; up to 20M EUR / 4% turnover for violations of core principles, data subject rights, and transfer rules.
