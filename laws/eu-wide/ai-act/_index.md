## Summary

Regulation (EU) 2024/1689, the Artificial Intelligence Act, is the world's first comprehensive legal framework for AI. It establishes harmonised rules for the placing on the market, putting into service, and use of AI systems in the EU. The Regulation adopts a risk-based approach, classifying AI systems into prohibited, high-risk, and limited-risk categories, with obligations scaled accordingly. It entered into force on 1 August 2024, with prohibitions applying from 2 February 2025, obligations for general-purpose AI models from 2 August 2025, and high-risk system requirements from 2 August 2026.

The AI Act applies to providers placing AI systems or general-purpose AI models on the EU market, deployers within the EU, and third-country providers whose AI outputs are used in the EU. It covers importers, distributors, and product manufacturers embedding AI. Exemptions exist for purely military/defence/national security use, scientific R&D, open-source models (unless high-risk or prohibited), and personal non-professional use.

The Regulation bans outright certain AI practices deemed unacceptable: subliminal manipulation, exploitation of vulnerabilities, social scoring, individual criminal risk prediction based solely on profiling, untargeted facial image scraping, workplace/education emotion recognition (with medical/safety exceptions), biometric categorisation inferring sensitive attributes, and real-time remote biometric identification in public spaces for law enforcement (subject to narrow exceptions requiring judicial authorisation). High-risk AI systems -- those used as safety components of regulated products or in sensitive domains listed in Annex III (biometrics, critical infrastructure, education, employment, essential services, law enforcement, migration, justice) -- must comply with requirements for risk management, data governance, technical documentation, transparency, human oversight, accuracy, robustness, and cybersecurity, and undergo conformity assessment before market placement.

General-purpose AI models (including large foundation models) face their own obligations: technical documentation, downstream provider information, copyright compliance, and training data summaries. Models with systemic risk (presumed at 10^25 FLOPs of training compute) must additionally perform adversarial testing, assess and mitigate systemic risks, report serious incidents, and ensure cybersecurity. The AI Office within the Commission has exclusive enforcement authority over general-purpose AI model providers.

Enforcement is shared between Member State market surveillance authorities (for AI systems) and the Commission's AI Office (for general-purpose AI models). Penalties are tiered: up to EUR 35 million or 7% of worldwide turnover for prohibited practices; up to EUR 15 million or 3% for high-risk and transparency violations; up to EUR 7.5 million or 1% for supplying misleading information. SME-specific caps apply. Affected persons have the right to lodge complaints with market surveillance authorities and to obtain explanations of decisions made using high-risk AI systems.

## Key Provisions

- **Article 1 -- Subject matter.** Sets out the Regulation's purpose: harmonised rules for AI systems in the EU, protecting health, safety, fundamental rights, democracy, and the environment while supporting innovation.
- **Article 2 -- Scope.** Defines territorial and material scope. Applies to providers, deployers, importers, and distributors of AI systems in the EU, including third-country actors whose outputs are used in the EU. Excludes military/national security, pure R&D, and personal use.
- **Article 3 -- Definitions.** Defines "AI system" as a machine-based system operating with varying autonomy that infers how to generate outputs (predictions, content, recommendations, decisions). Also defines provider, deployer, general-purpose AI model, systemic risk, and 65+ other terms.
- **Article 4 -- AI literacy.** Requires providers and deployers to ensure sufficient AI literacy among staff operating AI systems.
- **Article 5 -- Prohibited AI practices.** Bans subliminal/manipulative techniques, vulnerability exploitation, social scoring, sole-profiling criminal risk assessment, untargeted facial image scraping, workplace/education emotion recognition, biometric categorisation for sensitive attributes, and real-time remote biometric identification in public spaces (with narrow law enforcement exceptions).
- **Article 6 -- Classification rules for high-risk AI systems.** Two pathways: (1) AI used as safety components of products requiring third-party conformity assessment under Annex I legislation; (2) AI systems in sensitive use cases listed in Annex III. Includes derogation for systems that do not pose significant risk.
- **Article 9 -- Risk management system.** Mandates a continuous, iterative risk management process throughout the lifecycle of high-risk AI systems, covering identification, analysis, estimation, and mitigation of risks.
- **Article 10 -- Data and data governance.** High-risk AI training, validation, and testing datasets must meet quality criteria including relevance, representativeness, and freedom from errors. Allows processing of special category personal data for bias monitoring under strict conditions.
- **Article 14 -- Human oversight.** High-risk AI systems must be designed to allow effective human oversight, including the ability to understand system capabilities, monitor operation, interpret outputs, and override or halt the system.
- **Article 15 -- Accuracy, robustness and cybersecurity.** High-risk AI systems must achieve appropriate levels of accuracy, be resilient to errors and adversarial attacks, and include cybersecurity measures.
- **Article 50 -- Transparency obligations.** Providers of AI systems interacting with persons, generating synthetic content (including deepfakes), or performing emotion recognition/biometric categorisation must ensure users are informed. Deepfake content must be labelled as artificially generated.
- **Article 51 -- Classification of general-purpose AI models with systemic risk.** Models are classified as systemic risk if they have high-impact capabilities; a presumption applies at 10^25 FLOPs of training compute. The Commission can adjust this threshold.
- **Article 53 -- Obligations for providers of general-purpose AI models.** Requires technical documentation, downstream provider information, copyright policy compliance, and public training data summaries. Open-source models (non-systemic) are partially exempt.
- **Article 55 -- Obligations for systemic risk models.** Additional requirements: adversarial testing, systemic risk assessment and mitigation, serious incident reporting, and cybersecurity protection.
- **Article 72 -- Post-market monitoring.** Providers of high-risk AI systems must establish systematic post-market monitoring to evaluate continuous compliance throughout the system's lifetime.
- **Article 73 -- Serious incident reporting.** Providers must report serious incidents (death, serious health harm, critical infrastructure disruption, fundamental rights infringements) to market surveillance authorities within 15 days, or 2 days for widespread incidents.
- **Article 86 -- Right to explanation.** Affected persons subject to decisions based on high-risk AI output that produce legal effects have the right to clear and meaningful explanations of the AI system's role and the main elements of the decision.
- **Article 99 -- Penalties.** Three-tier fine structure: up to EUR 35M / 7% turnover for prohibited practices; up to EUR 15M / 3% for high-risk and transparency violations; up to EUR 7.5M / 1% for misleading information. Lower caps for SMEs.
- **Article 101 -- Fines for general-purpose AI model providers.** The Commission can directly fine GPAI model providers up to EUR 15M or 3% of worldwide turnover for non-compliance.
